Objective

In this programming assignment, we want to verify our understanding of vectors, their applications we have learned in class, and start considering abstraction of behaviors (defining behavior as a class and instantiate at runtime when necessary.)

 

Note: this assignment is for both CSS451 and CSS551, please refer to the credit distribution for the actual submission requirements. Different functionalities are required for each class.

 

 

Approach

We will build a simple simulation application, one where we continuously generate TravelingBalls and manipulate LineSegments to simulate simple behaviors and interactions between these objects. Here is my attempt at implementation for your reference.

 

I will explain the exercise, hint, according to the order of my implementation.

 

First, the MainCamera, here are my settings:

·         Position: (0, 16.5, -20)

·         Rotation: (22, 0, 0)

·         Scale: (1, 1, 1)

·         Fields of View: 45

 

Second, the world box, consisting of four perpendicular planes: LeftWall, RightWall, BackWall, and Floor. All are basic grey-ish white color. Here are their respective transform settings:

·         LeftWall:               P(-17, 0, 12),        R(-180, 90, 0),      S(34, 34, 1) [Note: all my walls are Quad]

·         RightWall:            P(17, 0, 12),          R(0, 90, 0),            S(34, 34, 1)

·         BackWall:             P(0, 0, 24)             R(0, 0, 0),              S(34, 34, 1)

·         Floor:                     P(0, 0, 12)             R(90, 0, 0),            S(34, 34, 1)

 

Third, the AimLine. This LineSegment initially runs from the LeftWall towards the RighWall. There are corresponding LineEndPts that the user can click on and manipulate the AimLine endpoints. The AimLine endpoints must always stay on one of the four walls of the world box. Here are the details:

·         LineSegment: you should draw a scaled/rotated cylinder to represent the line segment defined by the endpoints. Display the line segment in gray-ish color.

·         LineEndPt: This is a scaled sphere S(2, 2, 2). A user can always left-mouse click on and drag a LineEndPt. Remember, LineEndPt always stay on the walls (Left, Right, Back, or Floor) of the world box.

·         AimLine: The cylinder width is 0.2 or, S(0.2, LineLength, 0.2)

 

Fourth, the TravelingBalls. These are simple spheres, S(1, 1, 1), that continuously moves from the begin towards the end of an AimLine. You must allow your user to control these parameters (please implement these as SlidersWithEcho and place these in one of the unimportant corner):

·         Interval: in seconds upon which a new TravelingBall is generated. The range is 0.5 to 4, default 1.

·         Speed: in units/second where the next TravelingBall will travels. The range is 0.5 to 15, default 6.

·         LifeSpan: in seconds where the next TravelingBall will be alive for. The range is 1 to 15, default 10.

When generated, a TravelingBall receives the current AimLine direction and the Speed and LifeSpan settings. Note, once generated, the Speed and LifeSpan parameters of TravelingBalls do not change. For this reason, it is entirely possible for a newly generated TravelingBall, with a faster speed and longer lifespan, to overtake and out-live previously generated ones.

 

Fifth, the BigLine. Similar to the AimLine, the BigLine also has two LineEndPts. The differences are:

·         LineWidth is S(1.9, LineLength, 1.9), just slightly less than of the LineEndPt so that the LineEndPt sphere is always visible.

·         As in the case of AimLine, the endpoints of BigLine will never leave their corresponding walls.

·         TravelingBall interaction: traveling ball does not travel on big-lines. We will use these lines in the next assignment.

·         Note: if you think about the design and behavior, AimLine and BigLine are identical. If you designed your solution properly, this is literally a few lines of code.

 

World Interaction:

·         Here is the logic of the left-mouse button (LMB) click, in priority ordering:

o    If hits a LineEndPt:

§  Selects the end point, turns the selected end point black

§  Drags the end point (and thus manipulate the corresponding line)

§  Until LMB release and turns the end point back to red

o    Else, If hits Left or Back wall

§  Left wall: creates a new horizontal AimLine at the (y, z) position from the mouse click

§  Back wall: creates a BigLine from (x, y, backwall.z) to (x, 0, -1f), where (x, y) are coordinate values from the mouse click

o    Else, if click on the line segment of any AimLine of BigLine, the clicked on is removed (including the reflected segment if one existed)

§  In my implementation, you can only delete a line when mouse click is in the floor region (e.g., you cannot delete a line on the wall). It is acceptable if you have a similar behavior.

o    Note: the above means, you cannot delete lines by attempting to click on the line over the Left or Back walls (try it, you will end up creating new AimLine or BigLine).

9.       Selecton: in my solution, I have three ray-cast layers: Wall, EndPoint, and Line. In my mouse-support, I iterate through the layers, e.g., when nothing is currently select, I always begin by casting in the EndPoint layer, if nothing selected, I then cast in the Wall layer, and I only try to delete when both of the previous did not ended up selecting anything. So, in many cases, I perform three separate ray-cast into different layers in one mouse-select update.
